11 Shocking Facts About Your Tap Water (Most People Don't Know #7)

Think your tap water is safe? These 11 facts might change your mind. From forever chemicals to outdated infrastructure, here's what's really in the water you drink every day.

Most of us turn on the faucet without a second thought. Water comes out, it looks clear, and it tastes fine — so it must be safe, right? The truth is far more complicated than that.

The United States has some of the most advanced water treatment systems in the world, but "advanced" doesn't always mean "adequate." Between aging infrastructure, emerging contaminants, and regulatory gaps, there are things in your tap water that might surprise you. Here are 11 facts every homeowner should know.

1. The Average Glass of Tap Water Contains 300+ Contaminants

300+
Contaminants detected in U.S. tap water by the Environmental Working Group's national drinking water database

According to the Environmental Working Group's extensive analysis of nearly 50,000 water utilities, more than 300 contaminants have been detected in the tap water Americans drink every day. These include industrial chemicals, agricultural pollutants, disinfection byproducts, and heavy metals.

What makes this especially concerning is that more than half of these contaminants have no enforceable federal health standards. That means they're legal to have in your water at any concentration — simply because regulators haven't gotten around to setting limits yet.

2. Nearly 45% of U.S. Tap Water Comes From Contaminated Sources

45%
Of U.S. tap water originates from source water that contains detectable levels of PFAS and other persistent pollutants

A landmark study by the U.S. Geological Survey tested tap water from both public supplies and private wells across the country. The results showed that nearly 45% of samples contained at least one type of "forever chemical" or other persistent organic pollutant at detectable levels.

While water treatment plants do remove many of these contaminants, the process isn't perfect. Some chemicals, particularly PFAS compounds, are extremely difficult to filter out with conventional treatment methods. The contamination often traces back to industrial discharge, military bases, and even firefighting foam used at airports.

3. Lead Pipes Still Deliver Water to 10 Million American Homes

10M
Homes across the U.S. still receive water through lead service lines, according to EPA estimates

Despite the well-documented dangers of lead exposure, an estimated 10 million homes and buildings in the United States still receive their water through lead service lines. These are the pipes that connect the water main in the street to the plumbing inside your house.

The crisis in Flint, Michigan brought national attention to lead in water, but Flint is far from alone. Cities like Newark, Chicago, and Milwaukee have hundreds of thousands of lead service lines still in use. Replacing them is expensive — often $5,000 to $10,000 per home — and federal funding hasn't been enough to address the full scope of the problem.

4. PFAS "Forever Chemicals" Have Been Found in 98% of Americans' Blood

98%
Of Americans have detectable levels of PFAS in their bloodstream, with drinking water being a primary exposure route

Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances — better known as PFAS or "forever chemicals" — have become one of the most widespread environmental contaminants of our time. These synthetic chemicals were used for decades in non-stick cookware, waterproof clothing, food packaging, and firefighting foam.

The nickname "forever chemicals" isn't an exaggeration. PFAS don't break down in the environment or in your body. They accumulate over time and have been linked to cancer, thyroid disease, immune system suppression, and reproductive issues. Drinking water is considered one of the primary routes of human exposure, and most standard water treatment processes weren't designed to remove them.

5. Chlorine Disinfection Byproducts Are Linked to Serious Health Concerns

600+
Disinfection byproducts can form when chlorine reacts with organic matter in water, many with unknown health effects

Chlorine is added to municipal water supplies to kill bacteria and pathogens — and it does that job well. The problem is what happens after. When chlorine reacts with naturally occurring organic matter in water, it creates disinfection byproducts (DBPs) like trihalomethanes and haloacetic acids.

Long-term exposure to these byproducts has been associated with an increased risk of bladder cancer, reproductive problems, and liver and kidney damage in multiple peer-reviewed studies. It's a frustrating trade-off: the very chemical that makes your water safe from bacteria may be introducing other risks.

6. Bottled Water Is Actually Less Regulated Than Tap Water

FDA vs. EPA
Bottled water is regulated by the FDA with less frequent testing requirements, while tap water falls under stricter EPA oversight

Many people switch to bottled water because they assume it's cleaner or more heavily regulated. In reality, the opposite is often true. Municipal tap water is regulated by the EPA, which requires hundreds of tests per month for large systems. Bottled water, regulated by the FDA, has far less stringent testing requirements.

In fact, roughly 25% of bottled water is simply repackaged tap water. Studies have also found that bottled water can contain microplastics from the packaging itself. Add the environmental cost of plastic bottles — 80% end up in landfills — and the economic cost (1,000 to 2,000 times more expensive than tap), and the case for bottled water gets thin quickly.

7. Your Water Treatment Plant May Be Running on Decades-Old Technology

50+ yrs
Average age of water treatment infrastructure in many U.S. cities, with some systems dating back to the early 1900s

This is the fact that most people find the hardest to believe. Much of America's water treatment infrastructure was built in the mid-20th century — and some systems date back even further. The American Society of Civil Engineers gave the nation's drinking water infrastructure a grade of C- in their most recent report card.

These aging systems were designed to handle the contaminants that scientists knew about 50 or 60 years ago. They weren't built to filter out PFAS, microplastics, pharmaceutical residues, or many of the other emerging contaminants that are now showing up in water supplies. Upgrading the nation's water infrastructure would cost an estimated $625 billion over the next 20 years.

8. Microplastics Have Been Found in 94% of U.S. Tap Water Samples

94%
Of tap water samples tested in the United States contained microplastic fibers, the highest rate of any country studied

A groundbreaking study by Orb Media found that 94% of tap water samples from the United States contained microplastic fibers — the highest contamination rate of any country tested. These tiny plastic particles, smaller than 5 millimeters, come from the breakdown of larger plastic waste, synthetic clothing fibers, and industrial processes.

The full health effects of ingesting microplastics are still being studied, but early research suggests they can carry toxic chemicals into the body, cause inflammation, and may disrupt the endocrine system. Most water treatment plants aren't equipped to filter out particles this small, meaning they pass directly through the system and into your glass.

9. Agricultural Runoff Is the #1 Source of Water Contamination in the U.S.

#1
Agricultural runoff is the leading source of water pollution in rivers and streams, and the second-largest source for wetlands

If you live in a rural or suburban area near farmland, your water supply faces unique challenges. Agricultural runoff — containing pesticides, herbicides, fertilizers, and animal waste — is the single largest source of water pollution in the country.

Nitrate from fertilizers is particularly dangerous for infants and has been linked to "blue baby syndrome." Atrazine, one of the most commonly used herbicides in the U.S., has been detected in drinking water supplies serving millions of Americans. While it's been banned in the European Union since 2004, it remains widely used in American agriculture and frequently shows up in water tests, especially during spring planting season.

10. Traces of Pharmaceuticals Have Been Detected in Drinking Water

56
Different pharmaceutical compounds were detected in drinking water supplies serving 41 million Americans in a major AP investigation

When people take medication, their bodies don't fully absorb all of it. The remainder gets flushed down the toilet and enters the wastewater system. Conventional water treatment plants weren't designed to remove pharmaceutical compounds, so trace amounts can end up right back in your drinking water.

An Associated Press investigation found 56 different pharmaceuticals and pharmaceutical byproducts in the drinking water supplies of 41 million Americans. These include antibiotics, anti-depressants, hormones, and painkillers. While the concentrations are extremely low — typically measured in parts per trillion — scientists are concerned about the effects of long-term exposure to this chemical cocktail, especially on children and pregnant women.

11. Water Quality Can Vary Dramatically From Block to Block

Varies
Water quality can differ significantly between neighboring houses depending on pipe age, material, and distance from treatment plant

Your neighbor's water and your water can be remarkably different — even if you're on the same municipal supply. The quality of water at your tap depends not just on what leaves the treatment plant, but on the condition of every pipe it travels through to reach you.

Older neighborhoods with lead or galvanized steel service lines will have different contaminant profiles than newer areas with copper or PVC pipes. Dead-end water mains can have higher disinfection byproducts because the water sits longer. Homes closer to the treatment plant may get different water than those at the end of the distribution system. The only way to truly know what's in your water is to test it at your own tap.

The Bottom Line

None of this means you should panic about your tap water. In most cases, U.S. municipal water is treated and safe to drink by current regulatory standards. But "meets current standards" and "is completely free of concerning substances" are two very different things.

The science on emerging contaminants like PFAS, microplastics, and pharmaceutical residues is still evolving, and regulations often lag years or even decades behind the research. Being informed is the first step toward protecting yourself and your family.

Key Takeaway

Your tap water is likely safe by current standards, but it almost certainly contains contaminants that aren't yet regulated. Testing your water at home is the single best step you can take. From there, you can choose the right filtration solution — whether that's a simple pitcher filter or a more comprehensive system — based on what's actually in your water.
