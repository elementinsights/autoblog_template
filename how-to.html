How to Install an Under-Sink Water Filter (Step-by-Step)

Installing an under-sink water filter is easier than you think. This guide walks you through the entire process in 8 simple steps — no plumber needed.

An under-sink water filter is one of the best upgrades you can make to your kitchen. It provides clean, filtered water on demand from a dedicated faucet — no more refilling pitchers or buying bottled water.

The good news? You don't need a plumber. Most under-sink filter systems are designed for DIY installation, and the entire process takes about 1-2 hours with basic tools. This guide walks you through every step so you can get it done right the first time.

What You'll Need

Before you start, gather everything in one place. There's nothing worse than being mid-install with water dripping and realizing you're missing a tool.

Tools

  • Adjustable wrench — for tightening supply line connections
  • Drill with 1/2" bit — for mounting the bracket and drilling the faucet hole (if needed)
  • Teflon tape — for sealing threaded connections and preventing leaks
  • Bucket & towels — for catching water when disconnecting the supply line

Materials

  • Under-sink filter system — includes filter housing, cartridge, and tubing
  • Dedicated faucet — usually included with the filter kit
  • Feed water adapter — connects your existing cold water line to the filter
Tip: Most quality filter systems (like the APEC ROES-50) include all the hardware, tubing, and fittings you need. Check what's in the box before buying anything extra.

Before You Start

A few things to consider before you begin the installation:

  • Check your water pressure. Most under-sink systems require 40-80 PSI. If your pressure is too low, the filter won't work efficiently.
  • Measure your cabinet space. Make sure the filter housing fits under your sink with room to spare for filter changes.
  • Identify your faucet mounting point. Many sinks have a pre-drilled hole (often covered by a soap dispenser cap or sprayer). If not, you'll need to drill through the countertop.
  • Read the manual first. Every system is slightly different. Skim the instructions before you start so you know what to expect.
Warning: If you have a granite or marble countertop, do not attempt to drill a faucet hole yourself. The stone can crack. Hire a countertop specialist for this step — the rest of the install is still DIY-friendly.

Step 1: Turn Off the Water Supply

Locate the cold water shut-off valve under your sink. It's typically on the right side, connected to the cold water supply line running up to your faucet. Turn the valve clockwise until it stops.

Once the valve is closed, open the cold water faucet above to release any remaining pressure in the line. Let it run until the water stops completely — this usually takes 10-15 seconds.

Tip: Place a bucket directly under the shut-off valve before disconnecting anything. Even with the water off, there's always some water trapped in the line that will drip out.

Step 2: Clear the Cabinet

Remove everything from under the sink — cleaning supplies, trash bags, all of it. You need clear access to the cold water supply line, the cabinet wall (for mounting), and the underside of the countertop (for the faucet).

Lay down old towels on the cabinet floor to absorb any water that drips during the installation. A headlamp or battery-powered light is extremely helpful here since under-sink areas are notoriously dark.

Tip: Take a photo of your existing plumbing setup before you start. If anything goes wrong, you'll have a reference for how it looked originally.

Step 3: Mount the Filter Bracket

Position the filter bracket on the inside wall of the cabinet. Most systems mount on the left or right wall, but choose whichever side gives you the most room and keeps the filter accessible for future cartridge changes.

Hold the bracket in place and mark the screw holes with a pencil. Drill pilot holes (this prevents the wood from splitting), then secure the bracket with the included screws. Snap or slide the filter housing into the bracket.

Warning: Make sure you mount the bracket at a height that allows you to easily remove and replace the filter cartridge. Leave at least 2-3 inches of clearance below the housing for cartridge removal.

Step 4: Connect the Water Line

This is the most critical step. You'll need to tap into your existing cold water supply line to feed water into the filter.

Disconnect the cold water supply line from the shut-off valve using your adjustable wrench. Install the feed water adapter (also called a tee fitting) onto the shut-off valve. Wrap the threads with 2-3 layers of Teflon tape before connecting — always wrap in the direction you'll be tightening.

Reconnect your existing supply line to one port of the adapter, and connect the filter inlet tubing to the other port. Hand-tighten first, then give each connection a quarter-turn with the wrench.

Tip: Don't over-tighten the connections. With Teflon tape, hand-tight plus a quarter-turn with a wrench is usually enough. Over-tightening can crack the fittings.

Step 5: Install the Dedicated Faucet

If your sink has a pre-drilled hole (check for a cap or unused sprayer hole), remove the cap and use that opening. If not, you'll need to drill a new hole in the countertop using the appropriate bit for your material.

Feed the faucet stem through the hole from above. From below, slide on the rubber gasket, the mounting plate, and the nut. Tighten the nut by hand until the faucet is snug and doesn't rotate.

Connect the tubing from the filter outlet to the faucet stem underneath. Push the tubing firmly into the quick-connect fitting until you hear or feel it click into place.

Tip: For stainless steel sinks, use a step drill bit (also called a unibit) for a clean hole. Standard drill bits can grab and tear the metal.

Step 6: Flush the System

With everything connected, it's time to turn the water back on — but do it slowly. Open the shut-off valve gradually (counter-clockwise) to let the system fill without a pressure surge.

Open the dedicated filter faucet and let it run for 5-10 minutes. The water may come out cloudy or have black specks at first — this is completely normal. It's carbon fines from the new filter cartridge being flushed out.

Continue flushing until the water runs clear and tastes clean. Some systems recommend flushing 2-3 gallons; check your manual for the specific recommendation.

Warning: Do not drink the water during the initial flush. The carbon fines are harmless but unpleasant. Wait until the water runs completely clear before using it for drinking or cooking.

Step 7: Check for Leaks

With the water running, carefully inspect every connection point. Start at the shut-off valve and work your way through each fitting: the feed water adapter, the filter inlet, the filter outlet, and the faucet connection.

The best method: place a dry paper towel under each connection and check it after 15 minutes. Even a tiny drip will show up clearly on the paper towel.

If you find a leak, turn off the water, disconnect the leaking fitting, add more Teflon tape, and reconnect. Most leaks are caused by insufficient tape or connections that aren't quite tight enough.

Tip: Check for leaks again after 24 hours. Some very slow drips don't show up immediately but will become obvious overnight. Place a dry towel under the system and check the next morning.

Step 8: Final Testing

Fill a glass from the dedicated faucet and evaluate the water. It should be crystal clear with no cloudiness, particles, or unusual taste. Compare it side-by-side with an unfiltered glass of tap water — you should notice a significant difference in taste and odor.

If you have a TDS (Total Dissolved Solids) meter, test both your tap water and filtered water. A reverse osmosis system should reduce TDS by 90-99%. A standard carbon filter won't change TDS much but will remove chlorine taste and odor.

Finally, mark your calendar for the next filter change. Most under-sink filters need replacement every 6-12 months depending on your water quality and usage.

Tip: Write the installation date on the filter housing with a permanent marker. This makes it easy to track when the filter needs replacing without trying to remember.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Low water flow from the filter faucet

This is the most common issue after installation. Check that all tubing is free of kinks, the shut-off valve is fully open, and your home water pressure is at least 40 PSI. If you have an RO system, the storage tank may need 2-4 hours to fill completely before you'll get full flow.

Water tastes strange or has an odor

If the filtered water has a plastic or chemical taste, you likely didn't flush the system long enough. Run the faucet for another 10-15 minutes. If the taste persists after flushing 5+ gallons, the filter cartridge may be defective — contact the manufacturer for a replacement.

Leaking at connections

Turn off the water, dry the connection thoroughly, and reapply Teflon tape (3-4 wraps this time). Make sure you're wrapping the tape in the correct direction — clockwise when looking at the end of the fitting. If a quick-connect fitting leaks, make sure the tubing is cut cleanly and pushed in all the way until it clicks.

Filter faucet drips when turned off

A dripping faucet usually means the faucet handle wasn't tightened enough during installation, or the internal O-ring is misaligned. Remove the faucet handle, check the O-ring, and reassemble. If the problem persists, the faucet may be defective.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a plumber to install an under-sink water filter?

No. Most under-sink filter systems are specifically designed for DIY installation. If you can use a wrench and a drill, you can install one yourself in 1-2 hours. The only exception is if you need to drill through granite, marble, or another hard countertop material — in that case, hire a countertop specialist for just the drilling step.

Will an under-sink filter reduce my water pressure?

The filter supplies water through its own dedicated faucet, so it has no effect on your main kitchen faucet's pressure. The dedicated faucet will have a slightly lower flow rate than your main faucet (typically 0.5-1.0 GPM), which is normal and by design — slower flow means better filtration.

How often do I need to replace the filter cartridge?

Most under-sink filters need replacement every 6-12 months, depending on your water quality and daily usage. RO systems have multiple stages with different replacement schedules: sediment and carbon pre-filters every 6-12 months, the RO membrane every 2-3 years. Your system's manual will have specific recommendations.

Summary

Installing an under-sink water filter is a straightforward DIY project that pays for itself quickly in better-tasting water and savings over bottled water. The key to a successful installation is preparation — gather your tools, read the manual, and take your time with the connections.

Key Takeaway

The entire installation takes 1-2 hours and requires just three tools: an adjustable wrench, a drill, and Teflon tape. Turn off the water first, take your time with connections, use Teflon tape on every threaded fitting, and always flush the system for at least 5-10 minutes before drinking. Check for leaks twice — once immediately and again after 24 hours.
