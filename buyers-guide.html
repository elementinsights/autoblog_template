Shopping for a water filter should be simple, but it's anything but. Walk into any big-box store or search Amazon and you'll find hundreds of options — pitchers, faucet mounts, under-sink systems, whole-house units — each claiming to be "the best." How do you know which one is actually right for you?

That's exactly why we wrote this guide. After months of research, lab testing, and hands-on experience with dozens of filters, we've distilled everything you need to know into one comprehensive resource. Whether you're a first-time buyer or upgrading an existing system, this guide will help you make a confident, informed decision.

Key Takeaway

There is no single "best" water filter. The right choice depends on your water quality, living situation (own vs. rent), budget, and how many contaminants you need to remove. This guide helps you match those factors to the perfect filter type.

Do You Need a Water Filter?

Not everyone needs a water filter — but most people would benefit from one. Here are the signs that you should seriously consider getting a filter:

Signs You Need a Water Filter

  • Your water tastes or smells off — chlorine taste, metallic flavor, or sulfur ("rotten egg") smell are common indicators that your water could use treatment.
  • You have older plumbing — homes built before 1986 may have lead pipes or lead solder, which can leach into your drinking water.
  • You're on well water — private wells aren't regulated by the EPA, so you're responsible for your own water quality testing and treatment.
  • Your area has known contamination — check the EWG Tap Water Database to see what's in your local water supply.
  • You buy bottled water regularly — a home filter will save you money and reduce plastic waste significantly.
  • You have young children or are pregnant — developing bodies are more susceptible to contaminants like lead, nitrates, and PFAS.

When You Might Not Need One

  • Your water tests clean — if your annual water quality report shows all contaminants below EPA limits and you're happy with the taste, you may not need a filter.
  • You're on a modern municipal system — some cities have excellent water treatment. Check your Consumer Confidence Report (CCR) first.
  • You already have a whole-house system — if your home came with a built-in filtration system, an additional point-of-use filter may be redundant.

Types of Water Filters

Understanding the different types of water filters is the most important step in choosing the right one. Each type has distinct strengths and trade-offs. Here's a breakdown of the six main categories:

Pitcher Filter
Best for Renters
The simplest way to get filtered water. Fill it up, wait a few minutes, and pour. No installation required. Models like Clearly Filtered remove 365+ contaminants, while basic Brita models target 30+. Great for individuals and small households.
Price: $20-90 Filter Life: 40-100 gal Install: None Contaminants: 30-365+
Under-Sink Reverse Osmosis
Premium
The gold standard for home filtration. RO systems force water through a semipermeable membrane that removes up to 99% of dissolved solids, heavy metals, and contaminants. Requires under-sink installation and a dedicated faucet. Best for homeowners who want the cleanest possible water.
Price: $150-500 Filter Life: 6-12 mo Install: DIY (1-2 hrs) Contaminants: 1,000+
Countertop Filter
Best for Ease
Sits on your counter and connects directly to your faucet or operates independently. No permanent installation. Offers better filtration than pitchers with faster flow rates, though they do take up counter space. Ideal for renters who want more than a pitcher but can't modify plumbing.
Price: $50-200 Filter Life: 200-500 gal Install: Minimal Contaminants: 50-200+
Faucet-Mount Filter
Budget
Attaches directly to your kitchen faucet and filters water on demand. Turn a switch to toggle between filtered and unfiltered water. Compact and affordable, but not compatible with all faucet types. Good entry-level option for basic chlorine and taste improvement.
Price: $20-40 Filter Life: 100 gal Install: 2 min Contaminants: 30-70
Whole House Filter
Premium
Installed at your home's main water line, it filters every drop of water entering your house — including showers, laundry, and outdoor faucets. Ideal for well water or homes with significant sediment or chlorine issues. Requires professional installation in most cases.
Price: $300-2,000+ Filter Life: 3-10 years Install: Professional Contaminants: 50-77+
Gravity Filter
Budget Long-Term
Works entirely by gravity — no electricity, no plumbing, no water pressure needed. Pour water into the top chamber and it slowly filters down. Berkey is the most popular brand. Exceptional filter life (up to 3,000 gallons per element) makes it the cheapest per-gallon option available.
Price: $150-400 Filter Life: 3,000 gal Install: None Contaminants: 200+

Key Features to Look For

No matter which filter type you choose, these are the features that separate a great filter from a mediocre one:

  1. NSF/ANSI Certification — This is non-negotiable. An NSF certification means the filter has been independently tested and verified to remove specific contaminants. Look for NSF 42, 53, 58, or 401 depending on your needs.
  2. Contaminant Removal List — Don't just take the manufacturer's word for it. Look for a published list of exactly which contaminants the filter removes and by what percentage. Reputable brands provide this data from third-party lab testing.
  3. Filter Life & Replacement Cost — A cheap filter with expensive replacements can cost more over time than a pricier filter with longer-lasting cartridges. Always calculate the cost per gallon, not just the upfront price.
  4. Flow Rate — Measured in gallons per minute (GPM). A slow filter can be frustrating for daily use. Pitchers are inherently slow (5-15 minutes per batch), while under-sink systems deliver filtered water on demand.
  5. Filter Capacity — Measured in gallons. Higher capacity means fewer filter changes and lower long-term cost. Berkey's 3,000-gallon capacity dwarfs Brita's 120-gallon filters.
  6. Material Quality — Look for BPA-free plastics, food-grade stainless steel, and lead-free fittings. Cheap materials can leach contaminants into the very water you're trying to clean.
  7. Installation Requirements — Consider whether you can (or want to) install the system yourself. Pitcher and gravity filters need zero installation. Under-sink systems require basic DIY skills. Whole-house systems usually need a plumber.
  8. Filter Change Indicator — Some filters have electronic or mechanical indicators that tell you when it's time to replace the cartridge. This prevents you from unknowingly drinking inadequately filtered water.

Red Flags to Avoid

The water filter market has its share of misleading products. Watch out for these warning signs:

  • No NSF certification — If a filter can't show you an NSF or IAPMO certification, be skeptical. Claims like "lab tested" without specifying which independent lab are a red flag.
  • Vague contaminant claims — "Removes 99% of contaminants" means nothing without specifying which contaminants and at what levels. Demand specifics.
  • Alkaline water gimmicks — Filters that claim to "alkalize" your water for health benefits are largely marketing hype. The pH of your drinking water has minimal impact on your body's pH levels.
  • Magnetic or "structured" water — Products claiming to "restructure" water molecules or use magnets for purification have zero scientific backing. Avoid them entirely.
  • Suspiciously low prices — If a filter system costs $10 and claims to remove lead, fluoride, and PFAS, it almost certainly doesn't. Quality filtration media costs money to manufacture.
  • No published filter life data — If the manufacturer can't tell you how many gallons the filter lasts, you have no way to calculate true cost or know when replacement is needed.
  • MLM or direct-sales-only brands — Water filters sold through multi-level marketing schemes are typically overpriced for what they deliver. You're paying for the sales structure, not better filtration.

Certifications Explained

NSF/ANSI certifications are the gold standard for water filter verification. Here's what each one means:

NSF/ANSI 42 — Aesthetic Effects

Covers taste, odor, and chlorine reduction. This is the most basic certification. If a filter only has NSF 42, it will improve how your water tastes and smells but may not remove health-related contaminants.

NSF/ANSI 53 — Health Effects

Covers reduction of specific health-related contaminants like lead, mercury, asbestos, and volatile organic compounds (VOCs). This is the certification you want if your water has known contaminant issues.

NSF/ANSI 58 — Reverse Osmosis

Specific to reverse osmosis systems. Verifies that the system reduces TDS (total dissolved solids) and specific contaminants like arsenic, barium, and chromium. If you're buying an RO system, make sure it has this certification.

NSF/ANSI 401 — Emerging Contaminants

Covers "emerging" contaminants that aren't yet federally regulated, including pharmaceuticals, herbicides, pesticides, and PFAS (forever chemicals). This is a newer certification and indicates a filter is tackling the latest known threats to water quality.

Pro Tip: You can verify any filter's NSF certification at nsf.org. If a manufacturer claims NSF certification but their product doesn't appear in the NSF database, they're likely being misleading.

Where to Buy

Where you buy your filter matters — not just for price, but for warranty protection and authenticity:

  • Amazon — Largest selection and often the best prices. Use Amazon's "Subscribe & Save" for replacement filters to save 5-15%. Be cautious of third-party sellers offering prices that seem too good to be true — counterfeit filters are a real problem on the platform.
  • Direct from Manufacturer — Brands like Clearly Filtered, Berkey, and APEC often run promotions on their own websites. Buying direct guarantees authenticity and typically offers the best warranty support.
  • Home Improvement Stores — Home Depot, Lowe's, and similar retailers carry mainstream brands like Brita, PUR, and Aquasana. Good for seeing products in person before buying, and easy returns if something doesn't fit.
  • Costco / Warehouse Clubs — Occasionally carry water filter bundles at competitive prices. Great for stocking up on replacement filters in bulk.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know which contaminants are in my water?

Start with your annual Consumer Confidence Report (CCR), which your water utility is required to provide. For a more detailed analysis, use the EWG Tap Water Database or order a home water test kit ($15-30). If you're on well water, the EPA recommends testing annually for bacteria, nitrates, and pH at a minimum.

Do I need a water filter if I have city water?

Possibly. Municipal water meets EPA minimums, but "legal" doesn't always mean "safe." The EPA's standards haven't been updated for many contaminants in decades. The EWG has identified over 300 contaminants in U.S. tap water, many with health guidelines well below legal limits.

How much does it really cost per gallon to filter water?

It depends on the system. Berkey gravity filters cost about $0.02/gallon. Under-sink RO systems like the APEC ROES-50 cost around $0.08/gallon. Brita Elite pitchers cost roughly $0.09/gallon. Clearly Filtered pitchers cost about $0.14/gallon. Even the most expensive option is a fraction of the cost of bottled water ($1-2/gallon).

Can a water filter remove PFAS (forever chemicals)?

Yes, but not all of them can. Basic carbon filters (standard Brita) remove some PFAS, but not reliably. For thorough PFAS removal, look for filters with activated carbon block technology (Clearly Filtered), reverse osmosis systems (APEC ROES-50), or ion exchange resin. Check for NSF/ANSI P473 certification, which specifically covers PFOS and PFOA reduction.

Final Recommendations

Choosing a water filter doesn't have to be overwhelming. Here's our simple framework for making the right decision:

If you rent and want clean water with zero effort, get a Clearly Filtered Pitcher. It removes more contaminants than any other pitcher on the market and requires absolutely no installation.

If you own your home and want the cleanest water possible, install an APEC ROES-50 Reverse Osmosis System. The 1-2 hour DIY installation is worth it for 99% contaminant removal at just $0.08/gallon.

If you're on a tight budget, start with a Brita Elite Pitcher ($30). It won't remove everything, but it covers the basics and costs less than $10/month to operate.

And if you want whole-home filtration, the Aquasana EQ-1000 delivers filtered water to every tap for roughly a decade before needing replacement.

Whatever you choose, the most important thing is to start filtering. Even a basic filter removes chlorine, improves taste, and gives you one less thing to worry about when you pour a glass of water.

Bottom Line

Start with the Clearly Filtered Pitcher if you rent or want simplicity. Upgrade to the APEC ROES-50 if you own your home and want the highest level of filtration. Either way, you'll be drinking cleaner, safer water for a fraction of the cost of bottled.
